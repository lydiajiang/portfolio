Virtual Assist

Virtual Assist enables repair facilities and customers to video chat with their insurance companies.

Role

Product Designer

Company

Allstate

About the Product

This Agile XP product allows consumers and repair facilities to place a video call to an insurance adjuster, allowing insurance companies to virtually resolve claims. I designed the product’s iOS and Android app for repair facilities and mitigation vendors, a mobile web app for customers, and a desktop web app for auto adjusters, property adjusters, and their leadership.

The success of the product’s MVP release prompted Allstate to re-structure its location and employee strategy - the company opened operating centers nationwide, in which adjusters would be staffed to handle Virtual Assist calls. In one year, Virtual Assist went from handling 0% of Allstate’s millions of supplemental estimates to more than 70% nationwide.




Major Outcomes

Released MVP after 6 months of design and development time

Used to handle 70%+ of supplemental estimates nationwide

Cut a 1 week process down to 46 minutes

11,000+ repair facilities used app in less than 1 year (Feb 2018)

6 operating centers opened nationwide to handle VA calls

User and Business Problems

The insurance industry’s current claims process is marked by long cycle time, high operational cost, and poor customer experience.

One of its largest problems lies within the estimating sub-process: the estimated cost of repair to a customer’s car or property needs to be approved by an insurance company. This approval often takes a week, in which a paid adjuster is sent to visit the damaged car or property in-person. An auto adjuster may have to visit a damaged car multiple times in-person to approve any supplemental estimates (additional estimates submitted by the repair facility).

Solution

Iterative Design: The app’s design and feature set iterated together several times. The latest iteration included an onboarding process, which walked repair facilities and mitigation vendors through the process of inputting their information. This information would help connect their call to the correct adjuster. In previous iterations, user information was not saved, which I surfaced as a problem to prioritize in our backlog.

I was the product designer on an XP product team of one product manager and eight developers. We were tasked to reduce the inefficiencies in the estimating process by Allstate business.

Our product team’s MVP was an iOS mobile app for auto repair facilities, allowing them to place a video call to a queue of Allstate auto adjusters (who answered from our desktop application). I later designed the Android mobile app and mobile web app with the same feature set, which was released while the team refactored the desktop and iOS application.

User Research

Exploring the Problem Space: I recorded the sounds, locations, network connections, and lighting of multiple repair facilities to understand if there were risks to relying on video in our app. An MSO (multi-shop organization) facility might be outfitted with tablets and well-lit open floors, while local shops might have cramped, poorly-lit, and loud spaces.

To identify problems in the estimating process, I conducted exploratory research with auto repair facilities (focusing on owners and mechanics) and Allstate adjusters. I combined user interviews and contextual inquiries in order to identify user problems.

Repair facilities and insurance companies share a complex relationship which is rarely surfaced to the customers who bring their cars in - different types of estimates (initial, supplemental, total loss, full) and different types of shops (MSO, mom-and-pop, DRP) all factor into this relationship. I visited 25+ repair facilities over the course of the product’s one year development, surfacing their frustrations with having to wait on insurance companies for estimates. This information helped identify different user groups’ pains, prioritize the team’s backlog, and validate/invalidate business requirements and assumptions.

Design

Exploring the Problem Space: I recorded the sounds, locations, network connections, and lighting of multiple repair facilities to understand if there were risks to relying on video in our app. An MSO (multi-shop organization) facility might be outfitted with tablets and well-lit open floors, while local shops might have cramped, poorly-lit, and loud spaces.

I drove the design for the product’s iOS, Android, and desktop web app. One of the major goals of this product was to provide a white-label service, where Allstate would be only the first company of many to be connected to consumers and repair facilities. I had been provided a white-label style guide to work off of instead of the Allstate HIG, but this style guide was not actively maintained (for example, it utilized a gradient which had not been tested for accessibility).

I established with the team that our design goals were to maximize usability and functionality to the user. I iterated on the design, flow, and feel of the apps over time. The gradient was removed for a flat color which met AA accessibility. While much of the flow and layout of the Android and iOS versions remained identical to each other, I opted to make as many elements as native to each OS as possible.

User Testing

Testing Prototypes and the Production App: Running user tests always generated unexpected results. I observed users opting to use tablets instead of mobile phones, or using the video feature to discuss paper invoices instead of sending an email. Each round of testing resulted in iterations in the product’s design.

User testing was crucial to my design process. New features were prototyped and tested before being developed. I consistently tested our UAT environments before features were pushed into production, and I observed our app in production in the actual context of use after major releases.

One of the most challenging aspects of the product’s design was balancing design debt with immediate user value. As a team, we had chosen to focus on designing for the user and business problems which we could solve in the next few months - not the next year. This mindset, while it drove the product’s success, also lent itself heavily to design debt. User testing was able to inform when and why we should focus on long-term or short-term solutioning; as the product was iteratively designed and then released, I conducted regular user observations and user tests over the course of six months. Whenever I brought back poor usability as a finding of testing/observation for a short-term solution, the team knew a longer-term solution was the better path.

User Testing

From Left to Right: (1) I pair with the devs to check the quality of the design’s implementation; (2) User research (displayed on back wall) is discussed during product milestones (re-inceptions, re-scopings), and (3) I facilitated “design studios”, where all team members, regardless of design background, generate design ideas to solve a product problem.

Front-End Development: I regularly paired with the XP full-stack developers to understand technical limitations and unblock front-end stories, while coaching them in SASS and CSS. Understanding the basics of our codebase helped me prioritize the backlog when I paired with the PM, as well as identify design bugs in the production software. By pairing with the devs regularly, I was able to initiate other XP product teams to move towards using CSS compilers, recommending SASS as the industry standard.

Stakeholder Management: I became the representative for product design in the claims space, regularly demoing the product to stakeholders and potential clients with the PM and dev anchor. I became an architect for Virtual Assist’s future, which transitioned into architecting the claims space’s overall strategy. I was also a facilitator of the difficult discussions of its future (re-inceptions, scopings, iteration decisions, and kickoffs), where I recommended evolving our approach to user research to become more quantitative.

Next Steps

I was rolled off the team to spearhead the Photo Intake effort. When I rolled off, I identified the following next steps for the product and its design:



(1) Solidify consistency in the design system used across the Virtual Assist applications

(2) Establish unmoderated user testing and product analytics as an important driver of future designs

(3) Increase the marketability of the app through improvements in its visual design and feature set (push notifications)

(4) Abstract functionality to consume other virtual inspection methods and help other claims processes which have not been modernized
